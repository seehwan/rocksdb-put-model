Goal. Provide a coherent, end‑to‑end explanation of the put‑throughput model that matches real systems: why we extend the baseline, what each term means, how to fit parameters from RocksDB logs, and how to validate results.

1. Motivation & Problem Statement

The classic steady‑state model estimates sustainable put‑rate by dividing device bandwidth by per‑byte read/write requirements. In practice, we observed substantial gaps when: (1) compaction induces mixed read/write I/O penalties, (2) per‑level capacity & concurrency constraints arise, and (3) stall intermittently reduces effective duty cycle. The v2.1 model addresses these gaps.

2. Assumptions & Scope

  • Leveled compaction; background jobs and sub‑compactions may run concurrently.
  • Steady measurement window (size, level scores, and pending compactions near equilibrium).
  • Single storage device (or an aggregate treated as one) with calibrated pure read B_r and pure write B_w.
  • One consistent definition of WA_device (recommend: STATISTICS‑based device writes / user bytes).

3. Baseline Intuition

Baseline estimates treat the device as a single bottleneck: 

S_write = B_w / w_req
S_read  = B_r / r_req
S_mix   = B_eff / (w_req + η · r_req)

S_max = min(S_write, S_read, S_mix)

This is often optimistic because it ignores level‑specific limits and stall.

4. v2.1 Model — What Changes and Why

  1. Mixed I/O capacity: compaction performs reads and writes together. We model the effective capacity with a harmonic‑mix: 
    B_eff(ρ_r, ρ_w) = 1 / ( ρ_r / B_r + ρ_w / B_w )
  2. Per‑level capacity & concurrency: each level consumes a fraction of device writes and a proportional amount of reads. A level’s throughput is bounded by its parallelism and efficiency. 
    Device write bytes/s   = WA_device · S_put
Per‑level writes  W_l  = share_l · (WA_device · S_put)
Per‑level reads   R_l  = (read_to_write)_l · W_l

Global I/O:      Σ_l (W_l + R_l) ≤ B_eff
Level capacity:  (W_l + R_l) ≤ k_l · s_l · μ_eff_l · B_eff
  3. Stall duty: stall reduces the effective duty cycle. 
    S_put(final) = (1 − p_stall) · S_put(feasible)

6. Solving the Model

We search for the largest S_put that satisfies the global and per‑level constraints under a given mix. We cap the numerical search by B_eff and use ε‑guards for stability.

8. Sensitivity — What Moves the Needle

8.1 Stall duty

Higher stall directly scales down throughput. This guides L0 tuning and background job sizing.

8.2 Read/Write mix

When B_r > B_w, read‑heavier mixes can increase B_eff, hence S_put; adjust interpretation for your device.

9. Validation Checklist

  • Shares sum: Σl sharel ≈ 1
  • Mix sum: ρ_r + ρ_w = 1
  • Global I/O: Σl(W_l+R_l) ≤ B_eff at optimum
  • Per‑level I/O: (W_l+R_l) ≤ k_l·s_l·μ_eff_l·B_eff at optimum
  • Monotonicities: stall↑ ⇒ S↓, WA↑ ⇒ S↓

10. Practical Guidance

  • Use one WA definition per run; don’t mix LOG‑ and STATISTICS‑based WA in the same equation.
  • Calibrate B_eff against the actual compaction mix (ρ_r, ρ_w) you observe in the LOG.
  • Stall‑heavy regimes require careful L0 tuning and adequate background concurrency.
  • Model fitting: start with (read_to_write)_l ≈ 1.0, then refine with LOG and compression settings.

11. Limitations & Future Work

  • We treat μ_eff_l as a scalar efficiency; richer models could incorporate queueing delays, chunk distributions.
  • Mix (ρ) may vary over time; windowing or time‑weighted fits can improve fidelity.
  • Multiple devices require extending the capacity constraints per device and per level.
